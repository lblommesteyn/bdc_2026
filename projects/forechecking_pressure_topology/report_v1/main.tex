\begin{abstract}
Forechecking systems are usually described with discrete labels, but retrieval denial is created by continuous spatial pressure over time. We introduce \textbf{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}, a carrier-centric framework that measures pressure intensity, exit-lane availability, 3-second lane closure, and layered support from the first, second, and third forecheckers. Using all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games at 5v5, we segment 2{,}086 defensive-zone retrieval episodes and evaluate 14{,}921 tracking frames. Higher-pressure retrievals are consistently harder to escape: denial rates rise from 46.1\% in the lowest pressure decile to 64.1\% in the highest. RDT also predicts defensive-zone turnovers more accurately than simpler proximity-based baselines, improving area under the ROC curve from 0.640 for the nearest-defender model to 0.742 for the best topology model. We further provide coach-native archetypes, score-state splits, and team process benchmarking to support pre-scouting and clip review.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
This paper asks a direct question: \textbf{how does forechecking pressure influence defensive-zone retrieval failure, and can a spatial pressure-topology model predict turnovers more accurately than traditional proximity-based approaches?}

Forechecking decisions are often discussed as discrete systems (for example, 1-2-2 or 2-1-2), while execution quality is continuous and timing-dependent. On defensive-zone retrievals, the coaching problem is not only ``which system was called,'' but how quickly viable exit lanes are removed and whether pressure layers are synchronized. Here, \textbf{synchronized pressure layers} means the first forechecker pressures the puck immediately, while the second and third forecheckers arrive quickly enough to remove the carrier's next-best outlets before the play can escape. Prior work on zone entries and defensive structure has relied largely on event-level counts; the spatial geometry of pressure arrival is rarely modeled explicitly.

This paper uses a single organizing hypothesis: \textbf{retrieval denial is lane-removal dynamics}. We model this dynamic with a carrier-centric pressure topology and evaluate whether it is simultaneously interpretable for coaching and predictive for high-value defensive outcomes. The key claim is that a defender four feet closer to the carrier but with poor skating angle contributes less effective pressure than one six feet away but closing directly into the play, a distinction invisible to count-based approaches.

\section{Data and Episode Construction}
\subsection{Dataset}
The analysis uses all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games, combining synchronized tracking, event logs, and orientation metadata. The modeling unit is a 5v5 defensive-zone retrieval episode.

\subsection{Episode Segmentation and Outcome Definitions}
Episodes begin when possession is secured in the defensive zone under 5v5 and end at the first terminal condition: controlled exit allowed, forced dump-out, turnover in defensive zone, stoppage, or timeout guardrail. Segmentation is rule-based and fully auditable.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}
At each sampled frame, the likely puck carrier is inferred as the nearest offensive skater to puck location. Defender pressure at carrier location $x$ is:
\[
P(x)=\sum_i \exp\!\left(-\frac{\|x-x_i\|^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{ETA}_i(x)}{\tau}\right).
\]
This pressure score is our proposed modeling choice rather than a league-standard formula: it combines distance, estimated arrival time, and skating direction into one continuous measure of how disruptive each defender is to the carrier. The ETA term uses a \textbf{bounded-acceleration reachability approximation}, meaning we estimate how fast a defender can realistically reach the carrier if acceleration stays within physically plausible limits. The spread parameter $\sigma_i$ is direction-dependent: a defender skating toward the carrier has a tighter, more concentrated pressure footprint, while one moving across or away from the play has a broader, weaker footprint. This jointly rewards proximity, arrival speed, and directional commitment; all three are necessary for effective lane denial.

\subsection{Corridor Costs and Lane Closure}
Four exit corridors are defined: middle lane, strong-side boards, weak-side boards, and behind the net. For each corridor, the cost is computed by integrating the pressure field along the canonical exit path from the carrier's current position. The \textbf{cheapest corridor} (the minimum cost across the four options) represents the carrier's best instantaneous escape route. We summarize how pressure evolves with \textbf{3-second closure}, defined as cheapest-corridor cost at $t+3$s minus cheapest-corridor cost at $t=0$. Positive values mean the best lane became harder to use over the next three seconds; negative values mean the carrier found or preserved an easier exit. We use a 3-second window because it covers the first decisive forecheck wave in most retrievals and keeps the metric anchored to the same early-decision window across episodes.

\subsection{Episode-Level Features, Archetypes, and Validation}
Frame-level signals are aggregated to episode signatures: mean and peak pressure, corridor costs and closure, compactness, role-geometry distances and pressure shares (F1/F2/F3), D-pinch support count, and score-state context. Here, F1/F2/F3 denote the first, second, and third forecheckers in the pressure sequence. Archetypes are obtained by k-means ($k=4$) in the pressure-role feature space and labeled by their tactical character. The supervised prediction target is \textbf{turnover in defensive zone}, the most valuable denial event because it creates immediate possession gain. Denial rate (turnover + forced dump) is retained as the process lens for coaching-facing interpretation. Validation compares pressure-only and pressure+role models against nearest-distance and defender-count baselines using stratified cross-validated \textbf{area under the ROC curve (AUC)}.

Team-level TFPI (\textbf{Team Forecheck Pressure Index}) is defined as $\mathrm{TFPI}=100\cdot(\mathrm{TurnoverRate}+0.5\cdot\mathrm{ForcedDumpRate}-\mathrm{ControlledExitRate})$. Higher TFPI is better: it rewards retrievals that end in turnovers, gives partial credit to forced dump-outs, and penalizes clean exits against. We use a 0.5 weight because a forced dump-out is meaningfully better than allowing a controlled exit, but not as valuable as winning the puck outright with a turnover. \section{Discussion and Operational Deployment}
The central empirical finding is that denial is driven by \textit{lane-removal geometry}, not nearest-defender distance alone. Three action points follow. First, coaches should evaluate retrieval pressure by asking whether the carrier's best lane became harder within the first three seconds, not just whether F1 arrived on time. Second, layered support matters: the best denial outcomes occur when F2 and F3 remove secondary exits quickly enough to trap the carrier after first contact. Third, team benchmarking should focus on process quality, because the topology signal remains predictive even when score state is added and has only negligible incremental value.

In practice, this supports a clip-review workflow organized around (1) which corridor the carrier identified as cheapest at retrieval start, (2) whether that lane closed within three seconds, and (3) whether secondary support and D-pinch help arrived in time. Because all signals are computed per episode from tracking, the system supports line-matching, role accountability, and pre-scouting. A natural extension is to weight denial events by downstream transition value so that future versions of RDT distinguish between ``good'' dump-outs and truly damaging turnovers on a common value scale.

\section{Limitations}
Four constraints apply: role labels are inferred from positional proximity (nearest offensive skater to puck = carrier) and require manual video audit for confirmation; this inference is most susceptible to error in screening and tight-cycling sequences where multiple offensive players converge simultaneously, though such situations are less prevalent in the confined D-zone retrieval episodes studied here than in open-play cycling contexts, and the expected misclassification rate is modest relative to the episode sample. Acceleration and heading estimates inherit noise from the underlying tracking system. Corridor costs treat lane difficulty in a common pressure-based currency, but the four exits are not equal in downstream value; a future extension should weight lanes by the transition value they typically create when completed. Episode segmentation is rule-based rather than learned from tagged possession chains. The supervised endpoint is event occurrence rather than downstream expected-goal or transition-value outcome.

\section{Conclusion}
Forechecking success on retrievals is better modeled as a continuous topology of pressure, corridor availability, and closure dynamics than as a discrete system label. Retrieval Denial Topology produces interpretable coaching signals and improves turnover prediction over standard distance and count baselines. The practical message is simple: strong forechecks are layered forechecks, and the decisive window is the first three seconds after retrieval.
