\begin{abstract}
Forechecking systems are typically communicated with discrete tactical labels, but retrieval denial is produced by continuous spatial interactions over time. We introduce \textbf{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}, a carrier-centric framework that quantifies pressure intensity, corridor availability, first-3-second lane closure, and role-layer coordination (F1/F2/F3 with D-pinch support). Using all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games at 5v5, we segment 2{,}086 defensive-zone retrieval episodes and evaluate 14{,}921 tracking frames. The framework shows a monotonic pressure-response relationship for denial (46.1\% in the lowest pressure decile to 64.1\% in the highest), and materially outperforms nearest-defender/count baselines for D-zone turnover prediction (best AUC 0.742 vs 0.640 nearest-defender baseline). We further provide coach-native archetypes, score-state context splits, and team process benchmarking that support practical pre-scout and clip-review deployment.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} forechecking, puck retrievals, spatial topology, lane closure, hockey analytics

\section{Introduction}
Forechecking decisions are often discussed as discrete systems (for example, 1-2-2 or 2-1-2), while execution quality is continuous and timing-dependent. On defensive-zone retrievals, the coaching problem is not only ``which system was called,'' but how quickly viable exit lanes are removed and whether pressure layers are synchronized.

This paper uses a single organizing hypothesis: \textbf{retrieval denial is lane-removal dynamics}. We model this dynamic with a carrier-centric pressure topology and evaluate whether it is simultaneously interpretable for coaching and predictive for high-value defensive outcomes.

\section{Data and Episode Construction}
\subsection{Dataset}
The analysis uses all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games, combining synchronized tracking, event logs, and orientation metadata. The modeling unit is a 5v5 defensive-zone retrieval episode.

\subsection{Episode Segmentation and Outcome Definitions}
Episodes begin when possession is secured in the defensive zone under 5v5 and end at the first terminal condition: controlled exit allowed, forced dump-out, turnover in defensive zone, stoppage, or timeout guardrail. Segmentation is rule-based and fully auditable.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}
At each sampled frame, the likely puck carrier is inferred as the nearest offensive skater to puck location. Defender pressure at point $x$ is:
\[
P(x)=\sum_i \exp\left(-\frac{\|x-x_i\|^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\right)\exp\left(-\frac{\mathrm{ETA}_i(x)}{\tau}\right).
\]
The implemented pass uses acceleration-constrained reachability (bounded-acceleration ETA approximation) and anisotropic directionality (heading-aware spread and turning penalties).

\subsection{Core Definitions}
\textbf{Denial} is defined as turnover in defensive zone or forced dump-out. \textbf{Corridors} are middle lane, strong boards, weak boards, and behind net. The \textbf{cheapest corridor} is the minimum-cost instantaneous exit option. \textbf{3-second closure rate} is the drop in cheapest-corridor cost over the first three seconds of a retrieval.

\subsection{Episode-Level Feature Construction}
Frame-level topology signals are aggregated to episode signatures: local pressure and nearest-support structure, corridor costs by lane, first-3-second closure, compactness/funnel tendencies, role-aware support geometry (F1/F2/F3 distances and pressure shares), D-pinch support count, and score-state context.

\subsection{Archetypes and Validation Design}
Archetypes are obtained with k-means in pressure-role feature space ($k=4$). The supervised prediction target is \textbf{turnover in defensive zone}. Denial (turnover + forced dump-out) is used as the process lens for mechanism interpretation and coaching deployment.

Validation compares pressure-only and pressure+role models against nearest-distance and defender-count baselines using stratified cross-validated AUC.

\section{Discussion and Operational Deployment}
The central empirical result is that denial is best explained by \textit{lane-removal geometry}, not nearest-defender distance alone. In practice, this supports a deployment pattern where staffs pre-scout retrieval sectors with archetype tendencies, then tag clip review around first-3-second lane closure and F2/F3 layer continuity.

Because the framework is process-oriented and auditable, it is directly usable for line matching, role accountability, and within-team benchmarking under score-state context.

\section{Limitations}
The framework remains subject to four constraints: inferred role labels require manual video audit; acceleration and heading estimates inherit tracking noise; segmentation is rule-based rather than learned from tagged possession chains; and supervised validation targets event occurrence rather than downstream transition chance value.

\section{Conclusion}
Forechecking success on retrievals is better represented as a continuous topology of pressure, corridor availability, and closure dynamics. Retrieval Denial Topology produces interpretable mechanism-level signals and substantial predictive lift over standard distance/count baselines. The current system is suitable for practical coaching workflow integration and can be extended to transition-value modeling in future work.
