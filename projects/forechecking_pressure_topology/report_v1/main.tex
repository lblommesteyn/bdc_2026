\begin{abstract}
Forechecking systems are typically communicated with discrete tactical labels, but retrieval denial is produced by continuous spatial interactions over time. We introduce \textbf{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}, a carrier-centric framework that quantifies pressure intensity, corridor availability, first-3-second lane closure, and role-layer coordination (F1/F2/F3 with D-pinch support). Using all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games at 5v5, we segment 2{,}086 defensive-zone retrieval episodes and evaluate 14{,}921 tracking frames. The framework shows a monotonic pressure-response relationship for denial (46.1\% in the lowest pressure decile to 64.1\% in the highest), and materially outperforms nearest-defender/count baselines for D-zone turnover prediction (best AUC 0.742 vs 0.640 nearest-defender baseline). We further provide coach-native archetypes, score-state context splits, and team process benchmarking that support practical pre-scout and clip-review deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Forechecking decisions are often discussed as discrete systems (for example, 1-2-2 or 2-1-2), while execution quality is continuous and timing-dependent. On defensive-zone retrievals, the coaching problem is not only ``which system was called,'' but how quickly viable exit lanes are removed and whether pressure layers are synchronized. Prior work on zone entries and defensive structure has relied largely on event-level counts; the spatial geometry of pressure arrival is rarely modeled explicitly.

This paper uses a single organizing hypothesis: \textbf{retrieval denial is lane-removal dynamics}. We model this dynamic with a carrier-centric pressure topology and evaluate whether it is simultaneously interpretable for coaching and predictive for high-value defensive outcomes. The key claim is that a defender four feet closer to the carrier but with poor heading alignment contributes less effective pressure than one six feet away but closing on axis, a distinction invisible to count-based approaches.

\section{Data and Episode Construction}
\subsection{Dataset}
The analysis uses all 10 Big Data Cup 2026 public games, combining synchronized tracking, event logs, and orientation metadata. The modeling unit is a 5v5 defensive-zone retrieval episode.

\subsection{Episode Segmentation and Outcome Definitions}
Episodes begin when possession is secured in the defensive zone under 5v5 and end at the first terminal condition: controlled exit allowed, forced dump-out, turnover in defensive zone, stoppage, or timeout guardrail. Segmentation is rule-based and fully auditable.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Retrieval Denial Topology (RDT)}
At each sampled frame, the likely puck carrier is inferred as the nearest offensive skater to puck location. Defender pressure at carrier location $x$ is:
\[
P(x)=\sum_i \exp\!\left(-\frac{\|x-x_i\|^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{ETA}_i(x)}{\tau}\right).
\]
The ETA term uses a bounded-acceleration reachability approximation so that defenders who are physically closer but moving away contribute less. The spread parameter $\sigma_i$ is made anisotropic by the defender's heading: a defender aligned toward the carrier has a narrower, more intense pressure footprint, while one moving perpendicular or away has a wider, attenuated one. This jointly rewards proximity, arrival speed, and directional commitment; all three are necessary for effective lane denial.

\subsection{Corridor Costs and Lane Closure}
Four exit corridors are defined: middle lane, strong-side boards, weak-side boards, and behind the net. For each corridor, the cost is computed by integrating the pressure field along the canonical exit path from the carrier's current position. The \textbf{cheapest corridor} (the minimum cost across the four options) represents the carrier's best instantaneous escape route. \textbf{3-second closure rate} is the drop in cheapest-corridor cost between $t=0$ and $t+3$s of a retrieval: a large drop means defenders removed the best exit option quickly, which is the mechanism behind forced dump-outs and turnovers.

\subsection{Episode-Level Features, Archetypes, and Validation}
Frame-level signals are aggregated to episode signatures: mean and peak pressure, corridor costs and closure, compactness, role-geometry distances and pressure shares (F1/F2/F3), D-pinch support count, and score-state context. Archetypes are obtained by k-means ($k=4$) in the pressure-role feature space and labeled by their tactical character. The supervised prediction target is \textbf{turnover in defensive zone}, the highest-leverage denial event. Denial rate (turnover + forced dump) is retained as the process lens for coaching-facing interpretation. Validation compares pressure-only and pressure+role models against nearest-distance and defender-count baselines using stratified cross-validated AUC.

\section{Discussion and Operational Deployment}
The central empirical finding is that denial is driven by \textit{lane-removal geometry}, not nearest-defender distance alone. The coaching implication is direct: teams that generate high pressure volume but fail to close the cheapest corridor in the first three seconds will systematically allow more clean exits. In practice this supports a clip-review workflow organized around (1) which corridor the carrier identified as cheapest at retrieval start, (2) whether F2/F3 layers closed that corridor within three seconds, and (3) whether D-pinch support was active. Because all signals are computed per-episode from tracking, the system supports line-matching and role-accountability analysis under score-state context. A natural extension of this topology applies to zone entries from the offensive side, where corridor costs computed from the possessing team's perspective would yield a symmetric entry-success predictor and enable a unified spatial framework across both phases of the neutral-zone transition. Linking denial quality to downstream transition value would sharpen the outcome metric: a forced dump-out that cedes a controlled neutral-zone carry is materially different from one that produces an immediate D-zone turnover, and weighting denial events by the expected-goal delta of the subsequent possession chain converts RDT from a process metric into a decision-quality signal. For front-office evaluation, this chain from corridor-closure geometry to expected-goal consequences provides a principled basis for comparing forecheck line combinations on a common value scale, replacing event-rate proxies with a direct measure of how much structured pressure translates into sustainable possession denial.

\section{Limitations}
Four constraints apply: role labels are inferred from positional proximity (nearest offensive skater to puck = carrier) and require manual video audit for confirmation; this inference is most susceptible to error in screening and tight-cycling sequences where multiple offensive players converge simultaneously, though such situations are less prevalent in the confined D-zone retrieval episodes studied here than in open-play cycling contexts, and the expected misclassification rate is modest relative to the episode sample. Acceleration and heading estimates inherit noise from the underlying tracking system. Episode segmentation is rule-based rather than learned from tagged possession chains. The supervised endpoint is event occurrence rather than downstream expected-goal or transition-value outcome.

\section{Conclusion}
Forechecking success on retrievals is better modeled as a continuous topology of pressure, corridor availability, and closure dynamics than as a discrete system label. Retrieval Denial Topology produces interpretable mechanism-level signals and meaningful predictive lift over standard distance and count baselines. The framework is suitable for practical coaching workflow integration and can be extended to transition-value modeling in future work.
